(1) Firstly, an ancestor in the first generation carried two recessive alleles for the trait. Secondly, the second generations married with individuals who either express red hair or carry one recessive allele. These can explain the apparent horizontal pattern.

(2) Assume that *R* is the dominant allele, and *r* is the recessive allele. Since III-2 has red hair, her genotype must be (*rr*). III-1, who is Scottish and does not have red hair, is assumed to be heterozygous (*Rr*) with a 40% probability, or homozygous dominant (*RR*) with a 60% probability. Therefore, the probability that IV-1 will have red hair is calculated as follows:  
$40\% \times \frac{1}{2} + 60\% \times 0 = 20\%$

(3) According to the I-2, the II-3 must have at least one recessive allele r. While II-3 doesn't have red hair, the genotype of II-3 is (*Rr*). Similarly, individual II-5 is also (*Rr*). II-4 has red hair, so her genotype must be (rr). And given that III-9 does not have red hair, her genotype is (Rr). Since III-12 has red hair and II-6 does not, the genotype of II-6 is (Rr). Thus, the probability that III-10 is heterozygous (Rr) is $\frac{2}{3}$. Consequently, the probability that IV-2 will have red hair is calculated as:  $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{6}$

### 8
(1) Xist recruits silencing factors like SPEN, HDACs, and PRC proteins, leading to chromatin modifications such as DNA methylation and histone modification. This forms facultative heterochromatin and gene silencing, leading to physical condensation of the inactive X chromosome.

(2) **Features**:
XRR is enriched with key histone marks like H3K27ac (acetylation), which is associated with active transcription and enhancer regions. These modifications are necessary for the recruitment of cohesin that help in the 3D organization of the chromosome. And these regulatory elements is crucial to the activation and maintenance of Xist transcription.

**Function**:
1. Activation of Xist: XRR contains essential cis-regulatory elements including Ftx, Jpx and Xert, ensuring that Xist RNA is produced at the appropriate time and location.
2. Maintenance of XCI: XRR helps sustain the silencing of genes on the inactive X chromosome (Xi) by promoting chromatin modifications. For examples, XRR recruits the PRC2, which catalyzes H3K27me3.
3. Formation of X-megadomains
4. Prevention of spurious gene expression

(3) 
**In vitro experiments**: 
Deletions of the XRR region through CRISPR-Cas9 in XEN cells resulted in decreased Xist expression and the disruption of gene silencing. Specifically, the deletions led to a loss of H3K27me3 and a partial recovery of H3K27ac on the inactive X chromosome. These alterations were associated with the formation of X-megadomains. 

**In vivo studies**: 
XRRa deletions in early embryos showed a similar impact on Xist expression and X-linked gene silencing.
